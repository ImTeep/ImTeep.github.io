Auburn University Small Satellite Program

Command and Data Handling (Software Development) Team Lead for the TRYAD Cubesat Mission

The TRYAD satellites (two sats) will detect terrestrial gamma ray flashes (TGFs), which originate in high altitude lightning clouds, to determine their spatial structure. From the AUSSP Website: "With funding from the National Science Foundation, researchers at the University of Alabama in Huntsville (UAH) and at Auburn University (AU) are working together to build two CubeSats fitted with gamma ray detectors. The UAH team is developing the gamma ray detector using scintillating material. The AU team is developing two CubeSats capable of housing and handling the gamma ray detector. Once launched, the AU team will command and control the two satellites and UAH will plan the operations and analyze the data. The Goddard Space Flight Center is developing the light collector for the gamma ray detector. A high bandwidth radio will be used to transmit high data rates from the satellites to ground stations."

Summer 2019

Over the course of Summer 2019, I worked with the Auburn University Small Satellite Program (AUSSP) as the Command and Data Handling System (CDHS) team lead. My main focus was to produce software drivers to interface with on-board sensors and controllers. All the drivers were produced and tested in Python 2.7 over I2C on a BeagleBone Black. I successfully wrote drivers for 8 devices: LIS3MDL Magnetometer, MPU6050 Rate Gyro, INA219 Current Sensor, TMP100 Temperature Sensor, TCA9548A Multiplexer, MAX11644 Analog-to-Digital Converter (reaction wheel), PCA9530 Pulse Width Modulator and PCA9557 I/O Expander. These devices were set up on a large breakout board, all connected through the multiplexer which connects to the BeagleBone Black’s I2C bus. All these devices are able to be controlled and configured using basic commands which are documented in the drivers’ code themselves and in physically written documentation I made. Beyond just writing drivers for these devices, I constructed and tested a method for calibrating the LIS3MDL Magnetometers. The entire process of calibrating them have been thoroughly documented and should be easily reproduced.

Some of these devices are sensors, and only return detected values (magnetometer, rate gyro, current sensor, temperature sensor, and ADC), whereas some of these devices are controllers (multiplexer, PWM, and I/O). The PWM and I/O device are used to control the Actuator Assembly (reaction wheel & magnetorquer). I was able to connect the current actuator assembly model to the BBB and using the drivers for the PWM and I/O, I was able to control the speed/duty cycle, period, and direction of the reaction wheel.

2019-2020 Academic Year

I continued my work with AUSSP throughout Fall 2019 and Spring 2020. When the school year began, the program was able to recruit ~10 members onto the software team. As team lead, I was in charge of educating my team about the mission and Auburn's involvement in it, our role as the Command and Data Handling Subsystem, communicating important information from management to my team, and, of course, guiding my team on the work that needed to get done and how to go about reaching that goal.

I split up my team into 4 main subgroups, each focusing on a different area of software vital to the satellites' functions: my most experienced members worked on writing code in C for the satellites' microcontroller which mainly controls the satellites during detumbling but also acts as a middle-man for some sensors and controllers; another group worked on developing an orbit propogator which can generate it's own TLEs (Two Line Element) so the satellite could easily and accurately determine its position in orbit; another group continued my work on developing drivers and calibration techniques (in Python) for certain sensors; and the fourth group worked on drivers (in C - they would communicate with the microcontroller) that would read potentiometers and control servos so the satellites' fins could be actuated.

During this, I also chose one member of my team to train to be my successor as CDHS team lead because I was graduating at the end of the school year. In May 2020, I stepped down as CDHS team lead to focus on my future and other projects. Though I am no longer an official member of AUSSP, I remain committed to assisting them whenever needed.

My time in AUSSP provided me with invaluable experience and knowledge in Python coding, software development, (some) electrical engineering, and leadership. Other than the software skills I set out to obtain, I believe the experience of being part of a large team and collaborating closely with other students and professors alike will benefit me in my future. I thoroughly enjoyed working with the whole TRYAD team and absolutely loved being able to shepard my own team of students, both experienced and new to programming. I am exceedingly glad to have been able to be a part of the TRYAD mission and provide my effort towards something bigger than myself. I look forward to TRYAD's future and eventual launch.
